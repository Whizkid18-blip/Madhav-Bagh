The Stone Remembers

1892

In 1892, Shrimant Madhavrao Gaekwad, a cousin of Maharaja Sayajirao III of Baroda, built himself a mansion of red and white stone at the southern edge of the city. The arches are Indo-Saracenic. The jharokha balconies still catch the evening breeze. The corner tower has watched Vadodara grow up around it for over a hundred and thirty years.

Four generations of one family have lived inside these walls.

Morning

Light

The sun crosses this courtyard every morning, the way it has since 1892. The fountain runs quietly in the middle. The old swing creaks the way it always has. Somewhere inside, tea is being poured, and one of the cups is for you.

The Keepers

Home

Shivraj Gaekwad grew up under these arches. His grandfather built them. When age caught up with the old house, he and Indrayani Devi restored it one room at a time, and they chose to keep it a home rather than turn it into a hotel. Family photographs still line the corridors, and the family still lives here.

You don't check in at Madhav Bagh. You are welcomed in.

The First to Greet You

Stella

She will find you before anyone else does, unhurried and sure of herself, convinced the garden and everyone in it belong to her. These days she makes her rounds with Zephyr, her son, who is all long legs and big ideas. The family will tell you the house has four guest rooms. Stella behaves as though every lap in it is hers, and Zephyr is still working out which ones are his.

The first hello at Madhav Bagh usually arrives on four feet.

The Table

Rasoi

Indrayani Devi cooks from memory. Her recipes are royal Maratha, carried into the family by brides from Thanjavur, Gwalior, Kolhapur and Baroda, and refined over a century of family meals. Dinner might be a silver thali by lamplight. Breakfast might be waffles under the trees. Ask any guest what they remember most and the food usually comes up first.

Four Jewels

Sapphire

Room I of IV

Twin beds in carved wood, blue silk cushions, and a writing desk by the window. The quietest room in the house, and many guests' favourite.

Turquoise

Room II of IV

A sea green room under painted ceiling beams, lit by two chandeliers and a pair of Victorian lamps, with tall doors that open toward the courtyard.

Coral

Room III of IV

Warm coral walls with hand stencilled patterns, and the original brick archway framing the door. The brightest of the four rooms in the morning.

Ruby

Room IV of IV

A four poster bed under a high ceiling, exposed brick arches, and the deep red the mansion is known for.

Two Acres of Green

Wild

Two acres of garden in the middle of a working city. Mornings bring birdsong and evenings bring fireflies. Ten minutes away you can watch crocodiles on the Vishwamitri, or spend the day in the old city with its palaces, festivals and music. Wander as far as you like. Dinner is at eight.
